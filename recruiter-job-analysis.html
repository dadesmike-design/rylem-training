🎯 Why Job Analysis Matters
💡
The Foundation-to-Execution Bridge

You've learned what Rylem does. You've learned how to screen candidates. Job Analysis is what connects the two — it's the work you do BEFORE you source a single name.

  • You can't find the right person if you don't understand the job. Sourcing without analysis is guessing with someone else's time.
  • Bad sourcing wastes everyone. You, the BDM, and the client all lose when the wrong candidates hit the submission queue.
  • The recruiter who understands the job better than the candidate does WINS. When you can articulate the role, the team, the opportunity — candidates listen. Clients trust you.
  • Quality > quantity, always. One perfectly matched candidate beats five spray-and-pray submissions every time.
  • Every minute you spend understanding the JD saves 10 minutes of sourcing the wrong profiles.
🧅 Breaking Down the JD — The 5-Layer Method
⏱️
Target: 10 Minutes Per JD

Run through all 5 layers before you touch CEIPAL or LinkedIn. This is your pre-sourcing ritual — every req, every time.

1️⃣ Layer 1: The Basics

Extract the structural facts first. This takes 60 seconds and eliminates a ton of sourcing mistakes upfront.

JOB TITLE
Exact title matters for Boolean search and LinkedIn targeting. Note variations (Sr. vs Senior, Analyst II vs Analyst).
LOCATION & WORK MODEL
Remote / hybrid / onsite — and if hybrid, how many days? Onsite city matters for commute radius sourcing.
CONTRACT TYPE
W2, C2C, or 1099? Contract, contract-to-hire, or direct hire? Duration? These filter your candidate pool immediately.
DEPARTMENT & REPORTING
What team does this person join? Who do they report to? This shapes the culture and scope of the role.
2️⃣ Layer 2: Must-Haves vs. Nice-to-Haves

Most JDs are written by someone who wants everything. Your job is to find the 3–5 things that actually matter.

  • If the JD lists 15+ requirements, assume only 3–5 are true dealbreakers.
  • Skills listed first, in bold, or repeated multiple times = must-have.
  • Skills buried at the bottom, preceded by "preferred" or "nice to have" = wish list.
  • The test question: "If a candidate had everything EXCEPT [X], would you still interview them?" — Ask this to your BDM before you source. Force them to rank.
✅
Pro Move

Build a two-column list: Must-Have | Bonus. Source for every must-have. Nice-to-haves are tiebreakers — never eliminators.

3️⃣ Layer 3: Experience Level

Years alone don't tell the story. Match the actual scope of experience — not just the number.

  • "5–7 years" + "manage a team of 10" = mid-senior. Not a junior with 5 years of IC work.
  • Too senior = auto-reject. A Director-level candidate won't take a senior IC role. They won't step down in comp or scope.
  • Too junior = verify hands-on depth. "3 years of experience" that's all internships and bootcamps is not the same as 3 years at a mid-size company running real deliverables.
  • Look at the responsibilities section, not just the requirements. Scope of work tells you the real level.
  • Cross-check against common titles: Junior → Mid → Senior → Lead → Staff → Principal → Manager → Director
🚨
Don't Skip This Layer

Submitting overqualified candidates wastes everyone's time and burns your relationship with the BDM. Experience level is your first sourcing filter — always.

4️⃣ Layer 4: The Hidden Job

The JD tells you WHAT. The hidden job tells you WHY, HOW, and WHO — and it's not in the document. This is where great recruiters separate from average ones.

  • Why is this role open? Backfill, growth, new project, reorg? Each has a different urgency and candidate profile.
  • What does the team look like? Team size, senior or junior-heavy, collaborative vs. independent? This tells you culture and day-to-day reality.
  • What does success look like in 90 days? If the client can't answer this, the role isn't scoped. That's a BDM conversation, not a sourcing problem.
  • What's the hiring manager like? Hands-on vs. hands-off? Technical vs. business-focused? Influences who will thrive there.
  • What's the urgency level? "Needed immediately" vs. "we're building a pipeline" determines how hard you push and how fast you submit.
🔑
These answers come from the BDM — not the JD.

Never start sourcing without a BDM sync. The context they have is worth 2 hours of JD analysis.

5️⃣ Layer 5: Compensation & Market Reality

If the rate doesn't match the market, you have a problem. Find out now — not after you've burned 3 candidates' time.

  • Know market rates for the roles in Rylem's verticals. Use LinkedIn Salary, Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and your own submission history as reference.
  • If pay rate is 30%+ below market → flag it immediately. Either the BDM negotiates up or you deprioritize this req.
  • Classic mismatch: "Senior Cloud Architect — $45/hr." Market rate is $80–$120/hr. That req will never fill.
  • For managed program roles: rate is fixed. Know it upfront and source only candidates who will accept it. Don't waste anyone's time.
  • For direct client roles: you have room to negotiate. Know the client's max and work within it.
💰
Rate Misalignment = Wasted Pipeline

If you can't realistically fill a req at the offered rate, tell the BDM now. Deprioritizing a bad req is not giving up — it's protecting your time for reqs that can actually close.

🚩 Red Flags in Job Descriptions
⚠️
Spot These Before You Source

A bad JD doesn't mean you can't fill the role — it means you need to resolve the ambiguity BEFORE sourcing. Every red flag is a conversation you need to have with the BDM.

🪣 Kitchen Sink JD
Lists 20+ requirements across multiple disciplines. Nobody has all of these. The client doesn't know what they want. → BDM needs a scoping call before you touch CEIPAL.
🦄 Unicorn Hunting
"10 years of Kubernetes experience." Kubernetes is ~10 years old. Unrealistic requirements = client hasn't hired for this role before. → Flag immediately.
🌫️ Vague Descriptions
"Rock star who wears many hats." No deliverables, no outcomes, no scope. You can't match a candidate to vibes. → Push back for specifics via BDM.
💸 Rate Misalignment
Pay rate is 30%+ below market. You'll burn candidates' time and your own credibility. → Either negotiate up or deprioritize this req.
🔄 Revolving Door
Role open 90+ days or re-posted. Something is broken — previous candidates fell off for a reason. → Ask what happened before sending anyone in.
❓ 10 Questions to Ask About Every JD
📋 Your Pre-Sourcing Checklist

Get answers to these BEFORE you source. You should ask these to your BDM in your 10-minute sync call.

  • 1. Why is this role open? New position, backfill, growth? This shapes urgency and candidate profile.
  • 2. What does the team look like? Size, structure, who they report to. Team dynamics matter to candidates.
  • 3. What does day-to-day look like? Not just responsibilities — actual daily work. What does this person do in a typical Tuesday?
  • 4. What's the interview process? How many rounds, who's involved, timeline? Set expectations with candidates upfront.
  • 5. What happened to the last person in this role? Promoted? Quit? Fired? New role? This tells you a lot about the environment.
  • 6. What are the top 3 non-negotiable skills? Force them to prioritize. If everything is critical, nothing is.
  • 7. What would make someone a "no" within 5 minutes of an interview? The real dealbreakers — not the ones on paper.
  • 8. Is there flexibility on rate/location/experience level? Know the boundaries before you present candidates outside the spec.
  • 9. How quickly do they need someone? Urgency = how hard to push. True urgency = daily check-ins.
  • 10. Are there other vendors working this req? Know your competitive landscape. If 5 agencies are on this, speed matters more than ever.
💬
Save This List

Keep this in your desk or pinned on your screen. Run through it every time you pick up a new req — even if you only ask 5 of the 10, you'll source smarter.

🔤 JD-to-Search Translation
🔎 Turning a JD Into a Sourcing Strategy

Once you've run through the 5 layers, you're ready to source. Here's how to convert your analysis into action.

  • Step 1 — Extract keywords: Skills, tools, certifications, industries, job titles (exact + variants). These are your search terms.
  • Step 2 — Build your Boolean string: Pull must-have skills from Layer 2 + exact/variant titles + location. (See Advanced Sourcing module for deep-dive.)
  • Step 3 — Choose your sources: CEIPAL first (always), then LinkedIn X-ray, job boards by vertical, GitHub for technical roles, etc.
  • Step 4 — Check CEIPAL for similar past reqs: Have we filled a similar role before? Those candidates may be available again.
  • Step 5 — Map to Rylem's verticals: Is this IT, Finance, Marketing, Creative, HR, or Admin? Different verticals = different sourcing channels.
🔗
Boolean Example

("Business Analyst" OR "BA" OR "Systems Analyst") AND ("Agile" OR "Scrum") AND ("JIRA" OR "Confluence") AND ("financial services" OR "banking") -contract -freelance

🤝 Working with the BDM
🗣️ The 10-Minute Pre-Sourcing Sync

Every new req gets a 10-minute sync with the BDM BEFORE you source. No exceptions. Here's what to cover:

📌"Here's what I understand about this role..."▼

Walk the BDM through your Layer 1–3 analysis. Show them you read it. Confirm your understanding is correct. Catch mistakes before they cost you sourcing hours.

⚠️"Here are my concerns about the JD..."▼

Share red flags you spotted. Is the rate misaligned? Is it a kitchen sink JD? Has it been open 90 days? The BDM needs to know — and they may already have context that resolves it.

🚀"Here's my sourcing plan..."▼

Tell them your plan: what you're searching for, where you're looking, your Boolean string. Get buy-in so you're aligned from the start — not submitting candidates in the wrong direction.

  • The BDM owns the client relationship — you own the candidate relationship. Respect that line.
  • The BDM has context the JD doesn't: client culture, hiring manager personality, urgency, office politics.
  • If the JD is unclear or the client hasn't scoped the role properly → BDM goes back to the client. You don't.
  • Never submit a candidate on a req you haven't discussed with the BDM. Even one.

How to Use These Exercises

📄 Sample JD #1 — Fully Worked: Senior Business Analyst
JD: Senior Business Analyst — Contoso Financial, Fortune 500 | Seattle, WA (Hybrid 3x/wk) | Contract W2 | 12 months | $70–$85/hr

Contoso Financial is seeking a Senior Business Analyst to support our Digital Transformation program. You will partner with cross-functional stakeholders to define requirements, document processes, and drive delivery across multiple workstreams.

Required:
  • 7+ years as a Business Analyst in financial services or banking
  • Expert-level requirements gathering: user stories, process flows, use cases
  • Agile/Scrum experience (SAFe preferred)
  • JIRA, Confluence, Visio proficiency
  • Stakeholder management at Director and VP level
  • PMP or CBAP certification preferred
Preferred:
  • Experience with core banking platforms (FIS, Temenos)
  • SQL/data querying background
  • MBA or graduate degree in related field
🔍 5-Layer Analysis
LAYER 1: THE BASICS

Title: Senior Business Analyst | Company: Contoso Financial (Fortune 500) | Location: Seattle, WA (Hybrid — 3 days onsite) | Type: W2 Contract | Duration: 12 months | Rate: $70–$85/hr | Department: Digital Transformation

LAYER 2: MUST-HAVES VS. NICE-TO-HAVES

Must-Have (true dealbreakers):

7+ years BA experience in financial services • Requirements gathering (user stories, process flows) • Agile/Scrum • JIRA + Confluence • Stakeholder management at Director/VP level

Nice-to-Have (bonus points):

SAFe cert • PMP/CBAP • Core banking platform knowledge • SQL • MBA

LAYER 3: EXPERIENCE LEVEL

This is a mid-senior role. "7+ years" + "Director/VP stakeholders" = someone with gravitas and political savvy. A solid BA who's been a hands-on Agile practitioner for 7–10 years in FS. NOT a fresh BA who just learned Agile. NOT a project manager pivoting to BA. NOT a business systems analyst without stakeholder experience. Target titles: Senior BA, Lead Business Analyst, Principal Business Analyst (but not BA Manager or Director of BA — likely overqualified).

LAYER 4: THE HIDDEN JOB (Questions for BDM)
  • Why is this open? Digital Transformation programs often mean backfill + new budget — which is it?
  • How many BAs on the program? Is this a lead role or one of many?
  • Who is the hiring manager — a Program Director or a PMO lead?
  • What does 90-day success look like? Delivering a requirements doc? Running a sprint cycle?
  • Is there a chance this converts to perm? (12 months with Fortune 500 = often a keep-or-let-go moment)
LAYER 5: COMPENSATION REALITY CHECK

$70–$85/hr W2 for a Senior BA in Seattle financial services — this is competitive. Market rate for this profile in the PNW is $75–$100/hr W2. The range is slightly under market ceiling but within range. Candidates at the top of the range ($90–$100+) will likely pass; candidates at $65–$80 target rate are your sweet spot. No red flags here — proceed to source.

📄 Practice JD #2 — IT: Cloud Engineer (Mid-Size SaaS)
JD: Cloud Infrastructure Engineer — Nexlify Inc., SaaS | Austin, TX (Remote) | Contract C2C/W2 | 6 months CTH | $85–$110/hr

Nexlify is a Series B SaaS company building the next generation of logistics software. We're looking for a Cloud Infrastructure Engineer to own our AWS environment, lead our migration from legacy EC2-based architecture to EKS, and build out our observability stack.

Requirements:
  • 5+ years cloud infrastructure experience (AWS required)
  • Strong Kubernetes/EKS experience (production workloads)
  • Terraform or Pulumi for IaC
  • CI/CD pipelines (GitHub Actions, Jenkins, or similar)
  • Prometheus, Grafana, or Datadog experience
  • Python or Bash scripting for automation
  • Experience at a startup or fast-scaling tech company preferred
  • AWS Solutions Architect certification a plus
🎯
Your Turn — Work Through the 5 Layers

Before checking with your manager: What are the must-haves? What's the real experience level? What questions do you have for the BDM? Is C2C or W2 likely here (hint: CTH + Series B startup often means they want to hire directly)? Is $85–$110/hr realistic for a cloud engineer in 2026 Austin TX remote? Write it out, then review with your manager.

📄 Practice JD #3 — Finance: Senior Financial Analyst (Healthcare)
JD: Senior Financial Analyst — PacificCare Health Systems | Portland, OR (Hybrid 2x/wk) | Direct Hire | $90,000–$110,000/yr

PacificCare Health Systems is a regional healthcare network with 4,200 employees and $1.2B in annual revenue. We're adding a Senior Financial Analyst to our FP&A team to support budgeting, forecasting, and executive reporting.

Requirements:
  • 5–8 years FP&A experience, preferably in healthcare or regulated industry
  • Advanced Excel (pivot tables, power query, complex modeling)
  • Experience with Workday Adaptive Planning or similar EPM tool
  • SQL for data extraction
  • Strong presentation skills — regularly presents to VPs and C-suite
  • BA/BS in Finance, Accounting, or Economics required; CFA or CPA a plus
🎯
Your Turn — Work Through the 5 Layers

Note: This is a direct hire role. Rylem's fee is 25% of base salary — $22,500–$27,500. What does that change about how you source and present candidates? What's the seniority level really asking for here? Is $90–$110K competitive for a Senior FP&A analyst in Portland in 2026? What questions about this role do you need the BDM to answer?
