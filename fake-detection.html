🚨 Spotting Fake Resumes & Profiles

Fraud is real and it costs time, money, and client trust. Learn to spot the signs before you waste a screen or damage a relationship.

⚠️ Read this before your next round of sourcing

📄 Fake Resume Red Flags (Indeed / Job Boards)

🚩 What to Look For Before You Even Call

These signals appear on the resume itself. If you see multiple flags, reject and move on — don't spend time investigating.

🚨
Trust Your Gut

If something feels off about a resume, it probably is. You don't owe a fraudster a phone screen. Move on.

  • Messy formatting, excessive typos — Professionals invest in their resume presentation. Sloppy formatting = either not serious or not who they claim to be.
  • Resume is unusually long — Padding with generic responsibilities to hide gaps or make thin experience look substantial.
  • Candidate email doesn't match their name — "john.smith@gmail.com" applies but the email is "techguru2029@yahoo.com." Red flag.
  • Name mimics "American" names but history doesn't match — Name is "James Wilson" but all education and early work history is from overseas with no explanation of when/how they immigrated or got authorization.
  • Unusual job hopping across different states — Someone claiming permanent residency but lived in 5 different states in 3 years with no military or corporate explanation.
  • Company listed doesn't match its description — Google it. If the company doesn't exist, has a different focus, or went out of business years ago — that's fabricated experience.
  • Phone area code doesn't match any listed location — Claims to be in Seattle but the number is a Nashville area code with no explanation. Always note the mismatch.

💼 Fake LinkedIn Profile Red Flags

🔍 Profile-Level Warning Signs

LinkedIn is the primary sourcing platform — it's also where fraud is most common. Check these before reaching out.

  • Stock photos, actor photos, silhouettes, nature pics, or company logos — Real professionals have real headshots. Run the image through Google reverse image search if you're not sure.
  • Incomplete profile with limited connections (<50) — A legitimate senior professional will have a developed network. Thin profiles with minimal connections are often newly created fakes.
  • Poor spelling and grammar throughout — Especially in the headline and summary. Real professionals proofread their public profiles.
  • Suspicious work history — Overlapping dates (two full-time jobs simultaneously), vague job descriptions with no specifics, companies that don't exist on LinkedIn.
  • "Actively seeking C2C opportunities" + no photo — This combination is almost always a fraud indicator. Be especially cautious with Corp-to-Corp setups without a verifiable face attached.
  • Profile created recently but claims 15+ years experience — LinkedIn shows "joined" date. A senior engineer with 20 years experience who joined LinkedIn in 2023 is suspicious.
💡
Quick LinkedIn Verification

Cross-reference their profile against their resume. Dates, companies, titles, and locations should all align. Discrepancies are a signal, not a coincidence.

📞 Warning Signs DURING Screening

🎙️ What to Listen For on the Phone

Some fraud makes it past resume review. These are the signals to catch during the call itself.

🔊 Audio Red Flags
  • Bad phone connection with background voices — could be an outsourced screener
  • Keyboard clicking sounds when answering technical questions — someone else is typing answers in real time
  • Long, unnatural pauses before answering basic questions about their own experience
💬 Language Red Flags
  • "We did" instead of "I did" — collective language when describing their own projects suggests someone else's work
  • Won't answer questions directly — deflects, over-explains, or pivots away from specifics
  • Can't explain their own resume projects in simple terms
🚨
The Drop-and-Reschedule Pattern

Candidate drops the call right when technical questions start, then asks to reschedule "due to connection issues." This is almost always deliberate. Do not reschedule — reject.

✅ What To Do When You Spot It

📋 The 4-Step Response Protocol
  • Resume looks fake → Reject immediately.
    Don't schedule the call. Don't "give them a chance." Your time is worth more than investigating a fraud. Mark as rejected in CEIPAL with a note.
  • Phone screen feels off → Drill into ONE project in detail.
    Ask: "Walk me through [specific project on their resume] — what was your exact role, what was the biggest challenge, and how did you personally solve it?" Real candidates answer this easily. Fraudsters can't.
  • Still suspicious → Request a video call. Camera on. Non-negotiable.
    If they resist a video call for any reason, that's your answer. Legitimate candidates don't refuse to show their face.
  • Document everything in CEIPAL.
    Add detailed notes to the candidate's profile so your teammates don't waste time on the same person. Include what you observed and why you rejected them.
🤝
Protect the Team

When you log fraud flags in CEIPAL, you're protecting every other recruiter on the team. One note saves five wasted screens. Always log it.
