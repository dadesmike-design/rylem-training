Technique 1

🔗 Flip Search / Link Search

Use Google's link: operator to find people whose web pages link to certification or professional organizations. If someone links to PMI's website, there's a good chance they have a PMP.

💡 How It Works

Think of it like a reverse lookup. Instead of searching for "PMP" in a resume, you find anyone whose page points to the PMP certification body. Google's link: operator surfaces pages that hyperlink to the domain you specify.

  • link:pmi.org — finds pages that link to the Project Management Institute
  • link:shrm.org — finds pages that link to SHRM (great for HR professionals)
  • link:cpa.org — finds pages that link to CPA certification bodies
  • Works with ANY professional organization's domain — use whatever cert matches your role
🔍 Power Combos: Link + Resume Search

Combine flip search with resume keywords to filter down to actual candidates.

PMP / Project Managers

  • link:pmi.org resume OR CV OR vitae
  • link:pmi.org "project manager" resume

HR Professionals

  • link:shrm.org resume OR CV "HR manager"
  • link:shrm.org "SPHR" OR "PHR" resume

Finance / Accounting

  • link:aicpa.org resume OR CV "CPA"
  • link:cfainstitute.org resume "CFA"
🚨 Common Mistakes That Break This Search
  • ❌ Wrong: link:PMP — PMP is not a domain. Must use the actual certification body domain like pmi.org
  • ❌ Wrong: link: pmi.org — Space after the colon kills the search entirely. No space, ever.
  • ✅ Right: link:pmi.org — No space, full domain, no http/www needed
Technique 2 — THE BIG ONE

🔭 X-Ray Technique

Think of an X-ray at the doctor — it shows you the "bones" of a body that you can't see with the naked eye. The X-ray technique does the same to any website: it reveals every internal page Google has indexed, including pages the site owner may not even realize are public.

⚙️ The Operator: site:

The site: operator restricts Google results to a specific website, subdomain, or top-level domain. It works at three levels:

Full Domain

  • site:microsoft.com
  • Returns all indexed pages from microsoft.com and all its subdomains

Specific Subdomain

  • site:msdn.microsoft.com
  • Returns only pages from the MSDN subdomain — much more targeted

Top-Level Domain (TLD)

  • site:.tech
  • Returns ALL pages from any .tech domain — cast a wide net across an entire TLD
🧪 X-Ray in Action

Here's a complete X-ray search targeting developer resumes on .tech domains:

site:.tech resume OR CV OR vitae "back end engineer" OR developer OR programmer

This finds resumes and CVs on .tech personal sites from people who describe themselves as back end engineers, developers, or programmers.

🔑 Why This Is Powerful

Many professionals build personal websites or portfolios with their resume posted. These pages are indexed by Google but buried in standard searches. X-ray surfaces them instantly. You're finding candidates who have publicly posted their experience but aren't actively applying or visible on job boards.

Technique 3

🌐 Top Level Domain (TLD) Sourcing

Every web address ends in a TLD — .com, .edu, .gov, etc. Understanding how to X-ray by TLD lets you target entire categories of the internet at once.

🇺🇸

US Standard TLDs

  • .com — Commercial (default US)
  • .edu — Universities & colleges
  • .gov — Government agencies
  • .mil — Military
  • .org — Organizations & nonprofits
Tip

.edu X-rays find student/faculty pages with research backgrounds. .gov and .mil are gold for cleared candidates and public sector specialists.

🌍

Country Code TLDs

  • .ca — Canada
  • .uk — United Kingdom
  • .de — Germany
  • .au — Australia
  • .in — India
Tip

Use country TLDs when hiring internationally or for remote roles. X-raying site:.ca surfaces Canadian talent that won't appear in standard US-centric searches.

⚡

Generic / Specialty TLDs

  • .tech — Tech professionals & portfolios
  • .dev — Developers (Google-managed)
  • .io — Startups & tech companies
  • .design — Designers & creatives
  • .ai — AI/ML focused companies
Best For Tech Roles

Tech professionals love .tech, .dev, and .io domains for personal sites. These TLDs are goldmines for software engineers and developers.

📋 Full TLD Reference List

IANA (the body that governs domain names) maintains a complete list of all valid TLDs: data.iana.org/TLD/tlds-alpha-by-domain.txt — bookmark this. When you're recruiting in a new country or niche, look up the right TLD and X-ray it.

Technique 4 — Must Know

💼 LinkedIn X-Ray — Why This Beats LinkedIn Recruiter

This is the most important technique in this entire module. Every LinkedIn profile lives at linkedin.com/in/username — which means Google has indexed them, and you can search them directly without paying for LinkedIn Recruiter.

🏆 Why Google X-Ray Is BETTER Than LinkedIn Recruiter
LinkedIn Recruiter
  • Algorithm prioritizes YOUR network first (connected, same school, same company)
  • Search quality has declined since Microsoft acquisition
  • Expensive — you're renting access to biased results
Google X-Ray
  • Completely ignores LinkedIn's network algorithm
  • Returns results based purely on profile content match
  • Free — uses Google's search, which you already have
⚙️ The LinkedIn X-Ray Formula
site:linkedin.com/in "[job title]" "[key skill]" "[location]"
  • All LinkedIn profile URLs follow: linkedin.com/in/username — that's why site:linkedin.com/in targets profiles only
  • Use LinkedIn's own location phrasing (e.g., "San Antonio, Texas Metropolitan Area" not just "San Antonio")
  • You can search for ANYTHING that appears on a profile: schools, certifications, skills, past companies, volunteer work, even keywords from their About section
🌍 Geographic Targeting

🇺🇸 US Profiles Only

  • site:www.linkedin.com/in
  • The www prefix knocks out all international subdomains (ca.linkedin.com, uk.linkedin.com, etc.)
  • Use this when you need domestic candidates only

🌐 International Targeting

  • site:ca.linkedin.com/in — Canada
  • site:uk.linkedin.com/in — UK
  • site:au.linkedin.com/in — Australia
  • Use the country subdomain to target exactly where you need
📋 Real Search Examples

Finance Specialist with Fraud Cert

site:linkedin.com/in "CPA" OR "accountant" "CFE" OR "fraud" "Notre Dame" OR "Iowa State"

Senior Software Engineer (US Only)

site:www.linkedin.com/in "senior software engineer" "Python" OR "Go" "AWS" "Seattle"

HR Manager with SHRM Cert

site:www.linkedin.com/in "HR manager" OR "human resources" "SPHR" OR "PHR" "Chicago"

Project Manager (Canadian, PMP)

site:ca.linkedin.com/in "project manager" "PMP" "Agile" OR "Scrum" "Toronto"
Technique 5

🏢 X-Ray Competitors & Organizations

Don't just X-ray job boards and LinkedIn. Go straight to the source — competitor websites, universities, and professional organizations often have employee and member directories indexed by Google.

🎯

X-Ray Competitors

Find employees at companies your client wants to poach from — or find talent working for competitors that have similar role requirements.

site:competitor.com "software engineer"
site:amazon.com "senior product manager"
site:oracle.com "database administrator"
🎓

X-Ray Universities

Student and faculty pages are often publicly indexed. Great for finding recent grads or academic researchers entering industry.

site:stanford.edu resume "computer science"
site:mit.edu CV "machine learning"
site:.edu resume "MBA" "finance"
📋

X-Ray Professional Orgs

Many professional organizations publish member directories or speaker bios that are publicly indexed. Pure gold for niche or certified talent.

site:shrm.org "speaker" "CHRO"
site:pmi.org "member" resume
site:acfe.com "CFE" "fraud examiner"
Technique 6

📱 Social Media X-Ray

Google indexes public social media profiles. The trick: keep these searches simple. Social profiles contain minimal info — city, state, job title. Don't over-engineer these searches.

🎵

TikTok X-Ray

Google-indexed TikTok profiles show basic info. But note: hashtag search WITHIN TikTok is actually more powerful here.

site:tiktok.com attorney OR lawyer
site:tiktok.com "financial advisor" "Dallas"
site:tiktok.com nurse OR "RN" "New York"
Better in App

For TikTok, searching hashtags inside the app (#attorney #lawyerlife) often yields better results than Google x-ray. Use both.

🌳

Finding Contact Info via Linktree

Many social profiles include a Linktree link in their bio. That Linktree often contains personal websites, email addresses, and other contact methods.

  • When you find a promising profile via social X-ray, check if they have a Linktree
  • Linktree pages are indexed — search site:linktr.ee "[name]"
  • Often reveals personal email, portfolio, GitHub, or other platforms
🎯 Social Media Sourcing Rule

Keep it simple. People only put basics on social profiles — city, state, job title, maybe industry keywords. Use 2-3 terms max. Don't paste a full Boolean string — you'll get zero results. Save the complex strings for LinkedIn and resume searches.

🏋️ Exercises: Build Your Search Strings

Don't just read this — practice it. Complete all five exercises before your next sourcing session. Write your search strings out, run them in Google, and note what you find.

📝 Exercise 1 — Flip Search

You're sourcing for a Finance Manager role that requires a CFA designation.

  • Build a flip search using the CFA Institute's domain: cfainstitute.org
  • Combine it with resume keywords and the job title
  • Run it in Google and screenshot the top 3 results
  • Bonus: Repeat with aicpa.org for CPA-designated candidates
Check Yourself

Your search should look something like: link:cfainstitute.org resume OR CV "finance manager" OR "portfolio manager"

📝 Exercise 2 — TLD X-Ray for Developer Portfolios

You're sourcing a Back End Engineer with Python and AWS experience.

  • Build an X-ray search targeting .tech and .dev domains for resumes and portfolios
  • Include the title and required skills in your search
  • Run it and find at least one legitimate portfolio or resume
  • Bonus: Try the same search against site:.io — compare results
Check Yourself

Example: site:.tech resume OR portfolio "back end engineer" "Python" "AWS"

📝 Exercise 3 — LinkedIn X-Ray Search

You're sourcing a Senior Data Analyst in the Chicago area with Tableau experience.

  • Build a LinkedIn X-ray using site:www.linkedin.com/in (US-only)
  • Include the job title, key skill, and location using LinkedIn's phrasing
  • Run the search and compare to what LinkedIn Recruiter shows you for the same role
  • Discuss: How are the results different? Do you see candidates that didn't appear in LinkedIn Recruiter?
Check Yourself

Example: site:www.linkedin.com/in "data analyst" "Tableau" "Chicago, Illinois" — Note: use LinkedIn's exact location phrasing

📝 Exercise 4 — Competitor X-Ray

Your client needs a Senior Product Manager with experience at a top tech company. They specifically want someone who's worked at Amazon, Google, or Meta.

  • X-ray at least TWO of these company sites for product manager profiles
  • Add seniority level and any required skills
  • Find at least one profile page or bio that surfaces a PM with the right background
Check Yourself

Example: site:amazon.com "senior product manager" "AWS" OR "Alexa"

📝 Exercise 5 — Full Search Stack (Capstone)

Pick an open req you're currently working. Build three different searches for it using three different techniques from this module:

Search #1

Flip search using the most relevant certification body for this role

Search #2

LinkedIn X-ray targeting US-only profiles with location + skills

Search #3

TLD X-ray targeting a specialty domain relevant to the role (e.g., .tech, .dev, .edu)

Goal

Compare candidate quality across the three searches. This is your baseline sourcing toolkit for every req going forward.
