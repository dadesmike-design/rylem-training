👤 Candidate Persona Builder

Stop sourcing by keyword. Build the persona first — know WHO you're looking for before you search for them.

🎯 Do this BEFORE you open LinkedIn or CEIPAL

💡 Why Personas Matter

🔍 The Problem With Keyword-First Sourcing

Most recruiters jump straight to keyword searching. That's backwards.

💡
The Persona Principle

Building a persona first means you know WHO you're looking for, not just WHAT skills to search. A keyword search finds resumes. A persona search finds the right person.

  • Keyword-only searches return hundreds of irrelevant results
  • A persona narrows your sourcing to the right pool immediately
  • It aligns your language — you'll write better outreach when you know the person
  • It makes your submissions stronger because you can articulate the fit

📋 The Persona Framework

❓ Answer These 10 Questions for Every Req

Work through these before you source a single candidate. This takes 15 minutes and saves hours.

  • What's the client's environment?
    Tech stack, team size, culture, pace. A 10-person startup and a Fortune 500 need very different candidates with the "same" skills.
  • What are the TOP 3 must-have skills vs. nice-to-haves?
    Force-rank them. If the client gives you 15 requirements, ask: "If you could only have 3, which 3?" This shapes your screening criteria.
  • Where is your ideal candidate in their career?
    Junior, mid-level, senior, or management track? Don't just default to "5 years experience" — think about career stage and trajectory.
  • What's the biggest project they should have tackled?
    What does a "great fit" resume actually look like? Define the project or achievement that signals they're ready for this role.
  • What are their professional goals?
    Growth, stability, leadership, technical depth? Someone optimizing for stability won't thrive at a fast-moving startup — and vice versa.
  • What benefits/compensation matters most to them?
    Remote flexibility, equity, base salary, bonus structure? Know this before you present the role so you can sell the right features.
  • What job titles would they currently hold?
    Titles vary wildly by company. A "Senior Developer" at one company is a "Staff Engineer" at another. Build a list of equivalent titles to search.
  • What drives them?
    Technology passion, company mission, compensation, work-life balance? This determines your outreach angle and what you emphasize about the opportunity.
  • What companies would they be coming from?
    Research the client's current employees on LinkedIn. What companies did they come from? That's your sourcing target list.
  • Geographic requirements — local, remote, willing to relocate?
    Nail this down early. Don't invest in a candidate who can't take the role. Get explicit clarity from the client on remote vs. on-site.

🏢 Rylem-Specific Application

⭐ How to Apply This Across Our Verticals
🖥️ IT Verticals
Look for candidates at similar-scale companies — not just big tech. A developer from a 200-person SaaS company is often a better fit than someone from Google.
💰 Finance & Admin
Prioritize industry experience over tool-specific skills. A finance professional who knows your client's industry will outperform someone who just knows the software.
🎨 Creative & Marketing
Portfolio and project scope matter more than years of experience. A 3-year designer who's run enterprise campaigns beats a 10-year designer who hasn't.
🏆 The Culture Fit Question
Always ask yourself: would this person actually thrive at THIS specific client? Skills can transfer. Culture fit is harder to fix after placement.
⚠️
Don't Just Match Skills — Match Context

The right candidate has the right skills in the right context for the right stage of their career. All three have to align.
